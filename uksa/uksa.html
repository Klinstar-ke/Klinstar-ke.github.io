Page 1: Scope of the analysis

  • Introduction

    • This analysis represents the total number of Kenyan students registered in Algerian universities during the academic year 2019/2020, including both continuing and newly admitted students in 2020. The dataset contains demographic and academic information about the students such as gender, wilaya (university region), course pursued, duration of study and student status during the study period.

    The research focuses on examining the distribution and demographic characteristics of Kenyan students in Algeria to understand their academic engagement across various courses and geographical locations. The dashboard titled Student Distribution and Demographics provides an overview of the student population structure and forms the foundation for further analysis on academic performance, completion rates, and course challenges.

    Objectives of the Study

    The main objectives driving this research include:

    • To identify the ideal undergraduate courses for Kenyan students in Algeria — those in which students are most concentrated or have higher completion rates.
    • To determine the challenging courses during their academic progression, particularly by identifying areas with lower representation or higher discontinuation tendencies.
    • To examine discontinuation rates among students, including those who may have extended their duration of study or failed to complete within the expected period.

      • This study, conducted in 2025, covers data on students enrolled in 2020, focusing on those whose academic status and course duration were known by the time of analysis.

      Scope and Limitations of the Study

      The research provides a descriptive analysis of the available student data and does not explore deeper causal factors affecting student performance or retention. Key limitations include:

    • The reasons behind student dropouts or discontinuations were not captured.
    • External factors such as language proficiency in French —a possible major influence on student performance—were not evaluated.
    • Incomplete or missing data limited the comprehensiveness of the analysis.
    • The analysis relied solely on the available administrative data without qualitative insights from the students.
      • Despite these limitations, the study offers a valuable foundation for understanding the demographic and academic landscape of Kenyan students in Algeria.

Science Technique and Medicine** have mixed completion durations, with 2–6 year spans, suggesting varying progress speeds among students. Insight: Courses with longer durations (especially Architecture and Pharmacy) could indicate either program structure differences or instances of academic repetition and deferrals.     • This comparative chart highlights the relationship between discontinuation rates and success rates across all courses: Courses such as Economics, Aeronautics, Biomedical Engineering, French, and Geology report 100% success rates and minimal to zero discontinuations. Dental Surgery (50%) and Dr. Pharmacie (60%) exhibit lower success rates, suggesting higher dropout or failure tendencies. Insight:

    Success rates are generally high across most disciplines, but health and engineering programs remain the most challenging. These findings emphasize the need for targeted academic support, particularly in specialized science courses where discontinuation is more pronounced.

    5. General Observations

    • STEM fields dominate enrollment, reflecting Kenya’s focus on technical and scientific scholarship opportunities in Algeria.
    • Medicine, Pharmacy, and Architecture have extended durations and moderate discontinuation rates, highlighting the demanding nature of these programs.
    • Economics and SNV emerge as most stable and successful courses, combining timely completion with strong academic outcomes.
    • Language-based and social science programs (e.g., French, Economics) display high completion and success rates, possibly due to lighter technical demands or better linguistic adaptation.

    Conclusion

    The Course Analysis Dashboard provides a deeper understanding of how Kenyan students perform across various Algerian university programs. Overall, the findings reveal a high academic success rate, particularly among students who manage to complete their courses. However, technical and medical fields present greater challenges, as evidenced by extended durations and higher discontinuation rates. The results emphasize the need for pre-departure academic preparation, language training, and continuous support systems to enhance completion rates and academic experiences for Kenyan students studying abroad.

    The Regional (Wilaya) Analysis Dashboard demonstrates that Kenyan students in Algeria achieve consistently high academic success regardless of region, gender, or institutional setting. These insights can inform educational partnerships and support programs between Kenya and Algeria to ensure continued student success, especially in challenging disciplines.
